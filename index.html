📖 Module 1
Plot & Structure
Understanding how stories are organized and sequenced.
📖
What is Plot?
"The plot is the main sequence of events that make up the story."
Think of plot as the backbone of a story — it's everything that happens, in the order it happens.
🔢
Plot Order
"Plot is the order of events in the story."
Not just what happens — but the sequence. The order matters for tension, suspense, and meaning.

"The plot usually follows a particular structure called Freytag's Pyramid."

💡 Gustav Freytag (1816–1895) was a German novelist who mapped the 5-part structure of dramatic stories.
🔺 Interactive Freytag's Pyramid

"Freytag's Pyramid has five parts: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, and denouement, also known as resolution."

1
Exposition
Background info, characters, setting introduced
2
Rising Action
Tension builds, conflict develops
3
Climax
The turning point — highest tension
4
Falling Action
Events wind down after climax
5
Denouement
Resolution — the story concludes
👤 Module 2
Characterization
How authors build and develop characters in stories.

"Characterization"

💡 Characterization is the craft of creating believable, complex characters in a narrative.
⭐ Protagonist
🦸
"One character is clearly central to the story with all major events having some importance to this character - he/she is the PROTAGONIST."
⚡ Antagonist
🦹
"The opposite of the main character is called the ANTAGONIST."
Static vs. Dynamic Characters
🧊
Static (Flat) Character
"Static, also called flat, means the character stays the same throughout the story. They do not change."
Example: A villain who is purely evil from start to finish, never showing remorse or growth.
StaticFlatUnchanging
🌱
Dynamic (Developing) Character
"Developing, also called dynamic, means the character changes."
Example: A character who begins afraid but gains courage by the story's end. Growth is key.
DynamicDevelopingChanges

"Characters can be described as static or developing. Static, also called flat, means the character stays the same throughout the story. They do not change. Developing, also called dynamic, means the character changes."

⚔️ Module 3
Conflict
The engine that drives every story forward.
"Conflict is essential to plot. Without conflict there is no plot."
Conflict is what makes a story worth reading. It creates tension, stakes, and meaning.
"Conflict is the struggle between two entities. The main character, also known as the protagonist, encounters a conflict with the antagonist, which is an adversary."

"The two types of conflict are:"

💡 Every conflict in fiction falls into one of two categories: External or Internal.
1
⚡ External Conflict
"External - A struggle with a force outside one's self."
The character fights something in the outside world: another person, nature, society, or fate. The battle is visible and physical.
"Character vs. character"
"Character vs. nature or natural forces"
"Character vs. society, culture, ideas, practices, or customs of other people."
"Character vs. the circumstances of life facing him/her."
2
🌀 Internal Conflict
"Internal - A struggle within one's self; a person must make some decision, overcome pain, quiet their temper, resist an urge, etc."
The battle happens inside the character's mind. Doubt, desire, guilt, fear — these are all internal conflicts.
💡 Module 4
Theme
The deeper meaning beneath the story's surface.

"Theme"

💡 Theme is what the story is really about — beyond characters and events.
💎
Definition of Theme
"The theme is the main idea, lesson, or message in the short story."
Ask yourself: what does this story teach us about life, people, or the world? That's the theme.
🔍
Theme is Hidden
"Theme is a hidden element, but incredibly important."
Authors rarely state the theme directly. You must infer it from events, characters, symbols, and outcomes.
🧩 How to identify a theme▼

Ask these questions: What does the protagonist learn? What is repeated throughout the story? What do the symbols represent? What happens to characters who act a certain way? The answer to these questions often reveals the theme.


Common themes include: love and loss, freedom vs. oppression, identity, good vs. evil, the cost of ambition, and the nature of reality.

🌍 Module 5
Setting & Style
Where, when, and how a story is told.

"Setting"

🌅
What is Setting?
"Setting is a description of where and when the story takes place."
Setting includes physical location, historical period, time of day, season, weather, and social environment. It shapes the mood and influences character behavior.

"Style"

✍️
Author's Style
"Style: The manner in which an author expresses himself or herself in writing."
Style is a fingerprint — no two authors write exactly alike. Sentence length, vocabulary, tone, and rhythm all make up style.
🗣️
Language & Voice
"The language that the authors use to convey their thoughts."
Word choice (diction), sentence structure, and imagery are the tools authors use to create their unique voice.
🏗️ Structure and Organization of Writing▼
"What are the structure and organization of writing? Is it in letter form, does it have paragraphs separating main ideas? Are there lots of dialogues or just one long straight narrative? What does the title of the story tell you about what to expect?"

Analysing structure means looking at how the text is physically arranged and how that arrangement creates meaning.

🎨 Figurative Language▼
"Figurative language."

Figurative language includes metaphors, similes, personification, and other devices that go beyond the literal meaning of words. It creates imagery and deeper meaning.

🔮 Module 6
Literary Devices
The tools authors use to create meaning, depth, and effect.

"The following are a few common literary devices."

📚
Allusion
"Allusion. An indirect reference to another artistic work or person, event, or place."
Example: Saying someone has a "Midas touch" alludes to the Greek myth of King Midas, who turned everything to gold.
🔭
Foreshadowing
"Foreshadowing. The use of hints or clues to suggest what will happen later in a literary work."
Creates suspense and anticipation. Look for unusual details, dark imagery, or ominous dialogue early in a story.
⚡ Irony — Interactive Explorer
Click each type to explore the definition and example.

"Irony. An implied contradiction between what is said and what is meant. Irony is used to suggest the difference between appearance and reality and between expectation and fulfillment."

💬 Verbal Irony
🎭 Dramatic Irony
🔄 Situational Irony
💬
Verbal Irony
"verbal irony is when an author says one thing and means something else."
"Verbal irony occurs when a narrator or character says one thing and means something else."
Example: It's raining heavily and someone says "What lovely weather!" They mean the opposite of what they say. This is also called sarcasm when used harshly.
🎭
Dramatic Irony
"dramatic irony is when an audience perceives something that the characters don't know."
Example: In a horror film, the audience sees the monster hiding in the cupboard — but the character cheerfully opens it. The audience knows more than the character. This creates tension and suspense.
🔄
Situational Irony
"situational irony is a contradiction between the expected result and the actual result or between appearance and reality."
Example: A fire station burns down. We expect fire stations to be safe from fires — the opposite happening is situational irony.
🪄
Symbolism
"Symbolism. The use of an object or action to mean something more than its literal meaning."
A dove symbolizes peace. A storm symbolizes turmoil. Authors use symbols to add layers of meaning without stating them directly.
👤
Personification
"Personification"
Giving human qualities to non-human things. Example: "The wind whispered secrets." Wind cannot whisper — this is personification.
"Australia did not like Hilary."
"Fate has found a home for him."
"Hilary's sun shone again."
👁️ Module 7
Point of View
The lens through which the story is told.

"The point of view of a story is usually the angle from which the author tells his or her story. It is usually expressed in either the first person, second person, or third person."

🎮 POV Simulator — Same event, different perspectives
I heard the news and felt my heart sink. The telegram trembled in my hands. Was this truly the end?
💡 Click a POV button to see the same scene from a different angle.
🙋
First Person
"In the first person point of view, the author or narrator tells his or her story."
Uses "I" and "we." The narrator is a character in the story. Intimate and personal — but limited to what the narrator knows.
👉
Second Person
"The second person point of view is rarely used in narratives."
Uses "you." Places the reader inside the story. Used in choose-your-own-adventure books and some experimental literary fiction.
🔭
Third Person Limited
"The third person point of view can be expressed in either third person limited or omniscient."
"In the third person limited, the narrator is usually not included as a character in the story."
Uses "he/she/they." Follows one character's perspective. The narrator knows only what that character knows.
🌐
Third Person Omniscient
"In the third person omniscient, the character is fully involved in the story."
The "all-knowing" narrator. Can see inside any character's mind, knows past and future. God-like perspective over the whole story.
🪟 Story Analysis 1
The Story of an Hour
By Kate Chopin — A deep dive into freedom, irony, and the inner life of Louise Mallard.

📖 About the Story

A woman receives news of her husband's death and, instead of only grieving, discovers a profound sense of freedom — only for that freedom to be cruelly taken away. The story takes place in about one hour.

~1 HourThird Person OmniscientInternal Conflict
🔺 Plot Structure
Exposition — News arrives
Louise Mallard learns her husband Brently has died in a railroad accident. She is described as having heart trouble.
Rising Action — Louise retreats upstairs
She sits in a comfortable armchair. She looks out the open window and begins to feel something strange approaching.

"Louise sits in a comfortable armchair near an open window."

"The tops of trees that were all aquiver with the new spring life."

"Blue sky showing here and there through the clouds."

"The delicious breath of rain was in the air."

⭐ Climax — Freedom realized

"She begins whispering: 'Free, free, free!'"

"The climax occurs when Louise fully understands that she is free from the restrictions of marriage and begins to imagine a new life where she can live for herself."

"Free, free, free!"

"The word 'free' is repeated three times." — The repetition emphasises the overwhelming nature of her feeling.

"The word 'free' is repeated three times."

Falling Action — She descends
Louise walks downstairs. She imagines the "elixir of life" ahead of her.

"Drink from the elixir of life"

Denouement — Brently returns

"The doctors say she died from 'the joy that kills.'"

"The doctors say Louise dies of 'joy that kills,' but the reader knows that it's from the shock and despair of losing her brief taste of freedom."

👤 Characterization
"Louise Mallard is the main character (protagonist) of the story."
"Louise Mallard is considered a round character."
A "round" character is complex, multi-dimensional, and capable of surprise — like a real person.
"Then she begins to feel relief and freedom."
This shift from grief to relief is what makes Louise a dynamic, round character. Her emotional journey is the heart of the story.
⚔️ Conflict

"Conflict is the struggle between opposing forces in a story. In this short story, the main conflict is internal."

🌀
Louise Mallard vs. Herself
"Louise Mallard vs. Herself"
"This inner struggle shows her desire for independence versus her expected role as a devoted wife."
⚡
Louise vs. Society / Marriage
"Louise Mallard vs. Society / Marriage"
"The story suggests that marriage and social expectations limit women's freedom."
💡 Theme

"Freedom and Independence (Main Theme)"

"The most important theme is a woman's desire for personal freedom."
"After hearing about her husband's death, Louise Mallard realizes she can live for herself and make her own decisions."
"Free, free, free!"
⚡ Irony

"The ending shows strong irony."

"The doctors say she died from 'the joy that kills.'"
The doctors think Louise died of joy at seeing her husband alive. The reader knows she died of despair at losing her freedom. This is dramatic irony — we know what the doctors don't.
🪄 Symbolism
🪟
The Open Window
"The window allows her to see the sky, trees, and the world outside, which symbolizes freedom and new life."
Represents escape from the confined role of wife.
🌤️
Blue Sky & Spring Trees
"The open window, the sky, and the rain symbolize freedom, possibility, and emotional release."
Nature reflects Louise's inner awakening and renewal.
✨
Elixir of Life
"The open window and open sky act as a metaphor for new possibilities, freedom, and the vast potential of life outside societal restrictions."
"Renewal, hope, and new beginnings."
"Represents freedom and opportunity."
🌍 Setting & Style
"The events of the story happen in about one hour."
"The story follows a chronological order, taking place over about one hour."
"The language of the story is simple, clear, and descriptive."
"The narration is a sophisticated Third-Person Omniscient point of view."
☕ Story Analysis 2
The Medicine (Hilary's Story)
A tale of greed, moral decay, and a 'perfect crime' gone wrong.

📖 About the Story

Hilary plots to murder his wealthy aunt by offering her a "medicated" drink. But the aunt knows exactly what he is doing — and drinks it anyway. A study in greed, irony, and moral collapse.

External ConflictThird Person LimitedSituational Irony
🔺 The Climax

"The climax is the turning point of the story."

Hilary presents the drink

"The Climax occurs when Hilary presents the 'medicated' drink to his aunt."

The aunt reveals she knows

"The aunt reveals she is fully aware of Hilary's murderous intentions and his selfish greed."

She drinks it anyway

"The aunt drinks the medicine anyway."

👤 Characterization
"Hilary is a round character."
Hilary is complex — driven by greed but also self-deception. He is not simply evil; he is morally compromised in a human way.
"Hilary has to convince himself that he is performing an act of kindness rather than a cold-blooded crime."
This self-deception is what makes Hilary a round character. He lies to himself about his motivations.
⚔️ Conflict

"The most prominent conflict is external."

Hilary's conflict is primarily external: he is scheming against his aunt. But there is also an internal element — his need to rationalise his actions as kindness.
💡 Themes
"Greed and Inheritance"
Hilary's desire for his aunt's money is the engine of the plot. Greed corrupts and distorts his sense of morality.
"Moral Decay and Suspense"
The story traces Hilary's moral collapse as he convinces himself murder is mercy. The suspense comes from wondering whether the "perfect crime" will succeed.
"The 'Perfect Crime'"
Hilary believes he has constructed an undetectable murder. The irony is that his victim knows exactly what he is doing.
🌍 Setting & Style
"The story follows a linear, chronological structure."
"Since the plot revolves around a 'mercy killing,' the imagery is heavily colored by the sickroom."
"Hilary perceives the world through a lens of evidence and procedure."
His cold, calculating mind-set is reflected in precise, methodical language throughout the narrative.
👁️ Point of View

"Third-Person Limited"

"The story is told from a third-person perspective, but the 'lens' is strictly limited to the protagonist, Hilary."

⚡ Situational Irony
"Situational Irony occurs when something happens which is totally different from what is expected."
"She agreed to drink the medicine and he lost her money."
Hilary expected to inherit by successfully poisoning his aunt. Instead, she knowingly drinks the medicine — and presumably changes her will, leaving him with nothing. The "perfect crime" backfires completely.
🌿 Personification Examples
🌏
"Australia did not like Hilary."
Australia (a place) is given a human emotion — dislike. This personification shows Hilary's bad fortune abroad.
🏠
"Fate has found a home for him."
Fate (an abstract concept) is personified as a being that actively finds things. Suggests Hilary is destined for this moment.
☀️
"Hilary's sun shone again."
The sun (nature) reflects Hilary's revived hope. His fortune improving is mirrored in the natural world.
"He felt like a sailor who had reached harbor."
This is a simile (comparison using "like") that shows Hilary's sense of relief and safety — a momentary calm before the storm of the climax.
🎭 Module 8
Poetry Analysis
The tools and techniques for analysing poems at a deep level.
💭
Theme of a Poem
"The central idea or underlying message of the poem."
Same concept as in prose — but in poetry it is often more compressed and symbolic.
🗣️
The Speaker
"The 'voice' talking in the poem (not always the poet)."
Never assume the speaker IS the poet. The speaker is a persona created by the poet.
🎙️ Tone
"Tone"
"The speaker's attitude toward the subject."
Tone = writer's/speaker's feelings. Angry, melancholic, joyful, sarcastic, reverent…
😌 Mood
"Mood"
"The feeling the poem creates in the reader."
Mood = the reader's emotional response. Eerie, uplifting, nostalgic, tense…

"Difference Between Tone and Mood"

💡 Tone = speaker's attitude (they feel it). Mood = reader's experience (you feel it). A poem with a sarcastic tone might create an amused mood in the reader.
🖼️ Types of Imagery
👁️
"Visual Imagery (Sight)"
Language that creates pictures in the mind's eye.
"Blue sky showing here and there through the clouds."
👂
"Auditory Imagery (Sound)"
Language that evokes sounds, music, or noise.
👃
"Olfactory Imagery (Smell)"
Language that evokes scent or odour.
"The delicious breath of rain was in the air."
👅
"Gustatory Imagery (Taste)"
Language that evokes flavour and taste.
🤲
"Tactile Imagery (Touch)"
Language that evokes physical sensation, texture, or temperature.
🎨 Figurative Language in Poetry

"Figurative Language"

"Non-literal language used to create deeper meaning."

"Allusion"
"Reference to history/literature"
A nod to something outside the poem — a myth, a historical event, another book — that adds layers of meaning.
"Diction (Word Choice)"
Every word a poet chooses is deliberate. Diction shapes tone, mood, and meaning. Ask: why THIS word and not another?
🎵 Sound Devices
🔤
Rhyme
"Rhyme"
"Definition: Repetition of similar sounds at the end of words."
Example: "cat / hat / flat" — end rhymes create musicality and can create patterns (rhyme schemes).
📊
Rhyme Scheme
"Rhyme Scheme"
The pattern of end rhymes, labelled with letters: ABAB, AABB, ABCABC. Sonnets use specific rhyme schemes.
🔡
Alliteration
"Alliteration"
"Definition: Repetition of initial consonant sounds in nearby words."
Example: "Peter Piper picked a peck." The repeated 'P' sound creates rhythm and emphasis.
🔊
Assonance
"Assonance"
"Definition: Repetition of vowel sounds in nearby words."
Example: "The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain." The long 'A' sound repeats.
💥
Onomatopoeia
"Onomatopoeia"
"Words that imitate natural sounds."
Examples: buzz, hiss, crash, splash, sizzle. The word sounds like what it describes.
🥁
Rhythm & Meter
"Rhythm & Meter"
"The beat of the poem."
Meter is measured in "feet" (iambic, trochaic, etc.). Rhythm is the overall musical quality of the poem.
🏗️ Structure & Form
"Structure & Form"
"Fixed forms (sonnet, haiku, free verse, etc.)"
Structure refers to how a poem is physically organized (stanzas, line breaks, length). Form refers to established poetic structures like the sonnet (14 lines, specific rhyme scheme), haiku (3 lines, 5-7-5 syllables), or free verse (no fixed rules).
👁️ Point of View in Poetry
"First Person"
"The speaker uses 'I' or 'we.'"
"Second Person"
"The speaker uses 'you.'"
"Third Person"
"The speaker uses 'he,' 'she,' 'they,' or character names."
🌐 Literary Context

"Literary Context"

⏳
"Historical period"
When was the poem written? Wars, revolutions, and social movements all shape a poet's themes and language.
🌍
"Cultural background"
The poet's nationality, religion, social class, and traditions influence imagery, symbolism, and themes.
👤
"Author's life (when relevant)"
Biographical context can illuminate a poem — but don't over-rely on it. The text itself is primary evidence.
