Element 1 · Short Story
Plot & Freytag's Pyramid
The plot is the backbone of every story. Understanding its structure helps you analyze any piece of fiction.
DEFINITION
The plot is the main sequence of events that make up the story.
DEFINITION
Plot is the order of events in the story. The plot usually follows a particular structure called Freytag's Pyramid.
KEY FACT
Freytag's Pyramid has five parts: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, and denouement, also known as resolution.

🌅 Exposition

🌅 1. FREYTAG'S PYRAMID: Exposition

Your story has to start somewhere, and in Freytag's Pyramid, it starts with the exposition. This part of the story primarily introduces the major fictional elements – the setting, characters, style, etc. In the exposition, the writer's sole focus is on building the world in which the story's conflict happens.
Your exposition should end with the "inciting incident" – the event that starts the main conflict of the story.

💡 Think of the exposition as setting the stage — introducing who, where, and when before the action starts.

📈 2. FREYTAG'S PYRAMID: Rising Action

The rising action explores the story's conflict up until its climax.
Often, things "get worse" in this part of the story: someone makes a wrong decision, the antagonist hurts the protagonist, new characters further complicate the plot, etc.

💡 This is where tension builds — each event makes the conflict bigger and harder to escape.

⛰ 3. FREYTAG'S PYRAMID: Climax

Of course, every part of your story is important, but if there's one part where you really want to stick the landing, it's the climax. Here, the story's conflict peaks and we learn the fate of the main characters.
Think of the climax as the "turning" point in the story –
The climax is the point of greatest tension or intensity in the short story. It is the point where events take a major turn as the story races towards its conclusion.

💡 The climax is the peak of the mountain — the moment everything changes and the story begins heading toward its end.

📉 4. FREYTAG'S PYRAMID: Falling Action

In falling action, the writer explores the aftermath of the climax.

💡 After the turning point, the story winds down — consequences unfold and the world begins to settle into a new shape.

✅ 5. FREYTAG'S PYRAMID: Resolution / Denouement

The resolution of the story involves tying up the loose ends of the climax and falling action.

💡 "Denouement" is a French word meaning "untying." The resolution unties all the knots the story created.

PROTAGONIST vs ANTAGONIST
One character is clearly central to the story with all major events having some importance to this character — he/she is the PROTAGONIST. The opposite of the main character is called the ANTAGONIST.

Element 4 · Short Story
Theme
The invisible heartbeat of every story — what it is really about beneath the surface events.
DEFINITION
The theme is the main idea, lesson, or message in the short story.
KEY INSIGHT
Theme is a hidden element, but incredibly important.
🔍

Hidden but Central

Unlike plot (which is explicit), theme is implicit. The author never states it directly — you must infer it from events, characters, conflicts, and resolution.

🌐

Universal Message

A good theme speaks to universal human experiences: love, loss, freedom, identity, justice, courage, or the passage of time.

📖

Multiple Themes

A story can have more than one theme — a central (main) theme and several secondary themes, all woven together through the narrative.

Element 5 · Short Story
Setting
Where and when the story takes place — and why that matters deeply.
DEFINITION
Setting is a description of where and when the story takes place. In a short story there are fewer settings compared to a novel.
📍

Place

The physical location of the story — a room, a city, a landscape. In short stories, there are typically one or two key locations that carry symbolic weight.

🕐

Time

The historical period, season, time of day, or era. Time shapes what characters can and cannot do — especially regarding social expectations.

🌡

Atmosphere

The emotional tone created by the setting. A stormy night creates dread; a sunny spring morning creates hope. Setting and mood are deeply connected.

Element 6 · Short Story
Style & Figurative Language
How an author uses language, structure, and literary devices to express meaning beyond the literal.
DEFINITION
Style: The manner in which an author expresses himself or herself in writing.
The language that the authors use to convey their thoughts.
What are the structure and organization of writing? Is it in letter form, does it have paragraphs separating main ideas? Are there lots of dialogues or just one long straight narrative? What does the title of the story tell you about what to expect?

Common Literary Devices

The following are a few common literary devices.
Allusion. An indirect reference to another artistic work or person, event, or place.

💡 Example: Saying someone is a "Romeo" alludes to Shakespeare's character — a symbol of romantic love.

Foreshadowing. The use of hints or clues to suggest what will happen later in a literary work.

💡 Like shadows before an object arrives — the author plants seeds early that bloom into meaning later.

Irony. An implied contradiction between what is said and what is meant. Irony is used to suggest the difference between appearance and reality and between expectation and fulfillment. There are three kinds of irony:
VERBAL IRONY
Verbal irony is when an author says one thing and means something else. What is said is actually the opposite of what is meant/intended. Verbal irony occurs when a narrator or character says one thing and means something else.
DRAMATIC IRONY
Dramatic irony is when an audience perceives something that the characters don't know. It is also the contrast between what a character or narrator says and what a reader knows to be true.
SITUATIONAL IRONY
Situational irony is a contradiction between the expected result and the actual result or between appearance and reality, or between expectation and fulfillment, or between what is and what would seem appropriate.
Symbolism. The use of an object or action to mean something more than its literal meaning. Authors use symbolism to convey messages poetically or indirectly, through their stories, making them more interesting and complex pieces.

💡 A rose is literally a flower, but symbolically it represents love, beauty, or fleeting life.

Element 7 · Short Story
Narrator & Point of View
The angle from which the author tells the story changes everything — what we know, what we feel, and what we trust.
DEFINITION
The point of view of a story is usually the angle from which the author tells his or her story. It is usually expressed in either the first person, second person, or third person.

1st Person

I heard the news and felt my knees go weak. My heart, already fragile, seemed to pause inside my chest. I walked to my room alone, sat by the open window, and let the tears come — and then something else came too, something I had not expected: a feeling like wide open air.

FIRST PERSON
In the first person point of view, the author or narrator tells his or her story; it is mostly used in autobiographical or eyewitness reports.
SECOND PERSON
The second person point of view is rarely used in narratives.
THIRD PERSON LIMITED
The third person point of view can be expressed in either third person limited or omniscient. In the third person limited, the narrator is usually not included as a character in the story. He or she is detached from the story; however,
THIRD PERSON OMNISCIENT
In the third person omniscient, the character is fully involved in the story. He or she is able to see everything that is going on in the minds of the characters and is able to tell the movement of the characters as they progress from stage to stage.
Critical Analysis · Story 1
"The Story of An Hour"
By Kate Chopin — A masterpiece of irony, freedom, and the psychology of marriage in the 19th century.
STORY CONTEXT
"The Story of An Hour" — Critical Analysis

Freytag's Pyramid – Plot Structure

Stage 1 · Exposition (Introduction)

Meeting Louise Mallard

We are introduced to Mrs. Louise Mallard, a woman with heart trouble.
Her sister Josephine and her husband's friend Richards bring the news that Brently Mallard has died in a railroad accident.
They tell her the news carefully because of her weak heart.
Stage 2 · Rising Action

The unexpected emotion

Louise cries immediately after hearing the news.
She goes to her room alone and sits by the open window.
She notices signs of spring and new life outside (fresh air, blue sky, birds singing).
Gradually she realizes she feels something unexpected: a sense of freedom.
She begins whispering: "Free, free, free!"
Stage 3 · Climax (Turning Point)

The moment of realization

The climax occurs when Louise fully understands that she is free from the restrictions of marriage and begins to imagine a new life where she can live for herself.
This is the most important emotional moment in the story.
THE CLIMAX EXPLAINED
The climax is the turning point of the story, when the main character clearly realizes something important that changes the direction of the plot.
In "The Story of an Hour," the climax occurs when Louise Mallard fully realizes that she is free after her husband's death.
While sitting alone in her room and looking out of the window, she suddenly understands her new feeling and begins to whisper: "Free, free, free!"
At this moment: Louise understands that her husband's death means personal freedom for her. She imagines a future where she can live for herself. Her emotions change from sadness to excitement and hope.
This moment is the emotional peak of the story, because it reveals the central theme: a woman's desire for independence.
Stage 4 · Falling Action

Descending toward the ending

Louise opens the door and joins Josephine.
She goes downstairs feeling confident and victorious about her new future.
Later, the story moves quickly to the surprising ending, when her husband returns alive.
Stage 5 · Resolution (Denouement)

The devastating twist

Brently Mallard suddenly enters the house alive.
He was not involved in the accident.
Louise collapses and dies.
The doctors say she died from "the joy that kills."
CHARACTERIZATION
1. Louise Mallard
Louise Mallard is the main character (protagonist) of the story.
Louise Mallard is considered a round character.
🦸‍♀️

Protagonist

Louise is at the centre of every event. Everything in the story revolves around her emotional journey — from grief to liberation to death.

MAIN CHARACTER
🌀

Round / Dynamic

She undergoes a profound internal transformation within a single hour — from grieving widow to woman who tastes freedom for the first time.

COMPLEX CHARACTER
CONFLICT OVERVIEW
Conflict is the struggle between opposing forces in a story. In this short story, the main conflict is internal.
Louise experiences a psychological struggle after hearing that her husband has died.
At first, she feels sadness and grief.
Then she begins to feel relief and freedom.
She struggles to understand these conflicting emotions.
This inner struggle shows her desire for independence versus her expected role as a devoted wife.
The story suggests that marriage and social expectations limit women's freedom.
Louise realizes that during marriage someone else's will was often imposed on her life, even if her husband was kind.
The most important theme is a woman's desire for personal freedom.
After hearing about her husband's death, Louise Mallard realizes she can live for herself and make her own decisions. She feels that marriage limited her independence.
"Free, free, free!" — This shows her excitement about having control over her own life.
The story suggests that marriage in the 19th century could restrict women's freedom, even if the husband was kind.
The ending shows strong irony. The doctors believe Louise died from joy, but the reader understands that she died because her freedom suddenly disappeared.
Most of the story takes place in the Mallards' house, especially in Louise Mallard's bedroom.
Louise sits in a comfortable armchair near an open window.
The window allows her to see the sky, trees, and the world outside, which symbolizes freedom and new life.
The events of the story happen in about one hour, which explains the title "The Story of an Hour."
The story is also set in the late 19th century, a time when women had limited freedom in marriage and society.
The atmosphere changes during the story:
BEGINNING
Sadness and shock after hearing the news of the husband's death.
MIDDLE
Hope and excitement as Louise realizes her freedom.
ENDING
Sudden shock and tragedy.
LANGUAGE
The language of the story is simple, clear, and descriptive, but it also expresses deep emotions and psychological ideas.
Chopin describes nature and the environment outside the window. These descriptions reflect Louise's emotional transformation.
STRUCTURE
The structure of a story is the way its events are organized and presented. In this short story, Chopin uses a tight, linear structure that reflects the psychological journey of Louise Mallard.
The story follows a chronological order, taking place over about one hour.

POINT OF VIEW IN THE STORY
In Kate Chopin's "The Story of an Hour," the narration is a sophisticated Third-Person Omniscient point of view, though it is "selective" or "limited" in how it focuses on the protagonist, Louise Mallard.
The narrator stands outside the action but has access to the internal thoughts and feelings of the characters—specifically Louise.
🔭

Third-Person Omniscient (Selective)

The narrator is outside the story — not a character — but can read Louise's mind completely, sharing her most private thoughts and feelings with the reader.

🎯

Strategic Focus

Though omniscient, the narrator chooses to focus almost entirely on Louise — creating intimacy with her psychology while keeping other characters at a distance.

Critical Analysis · Story 2
"Hilary's Aunt"
A dark, suspenseful mystery about greed, inheritance, and a shocking twist — where the intended victim has the last word.

Freytag's Pyramid – Plot Structure

Stage 1 · Exposition (Introduction)

Setting the scene

We are introduced to Hilary and his ailing aunt — and his desperate financial situation.

Stage 2 · Rising Action

The deadly plan forms

INCITING INCIDENT
The doctor informs Hilary that his aunt is terminally ill.
Stage 3 · Climax (Turning Point)

The shocking confrontation

The Climax occurs when Hilary presents the "medicated" drink to his aunt.
THE TWIST
The aunt reveals she is fully aware of Hilary's murderous intentions and his selfish greed. She proves to be far more intelligent than he imagined.
The climax is the turning point of the story, when the main character clearly realizes something important that changes the direction of the plot.
Stage 4 · Falling Action

The aunt's extraordinary choice

Despite knowing it is poisoned, the aunt drinks the medicine anyway.
She consciously decides to take the overdose, turning the murder into a form of passive suicide, thereby denying Hilary the satisfaction of actively killing her.
Stage 5 · Resolution (Denouement)

Justice on her terms

Outcome: The aunt dies as planned, but not on Hilary's terms.
CHARACTERIZATION
Hilary is a round character because:
🎭

Hilary — Protagonist (Villain)

A complex, morally conflicted character who is both the story's protagonist and its primary antagonist force. He rationalizes his crime as kindness.

ROUND CHARACTER
👵

The Aunt — True Protagonist

Appears to be the victim but is revealed to be the wisest and most powerful character — she turns Hilary's murder into her own choice.

DECEPTIVELY POWERFUL
CONFLICT OVERVIEW
Conflict is the struggle between opposing forces in a story. In this short story, the main conflict is internal.
The most prominent conflict is external.
While the plot focuses on the execution of the plan, there is a subtle undercurrent of internal struggle within Hilary.
Hilary has to convince himself that he is performing an act of kindness rather than a cold-blooded crime.
Greed and Inheritance: The driving force behind Hilary's actions is entirely financial — he wants to inherit his aunt's wealth before she can change her will.
Moral Decay and Suspense: The story shows how financial desperation can corrupt a person's moral compass, creating psychological suspense.
LANGUAGE
The language
Ethical and legal terminology: Some references to law, morality, and medical practice highlight the constraints Hilary faces.
Highlights conflict: Conversations can emphasize Hilary's inner doubts or the pragmatic attitudes of others.
Develops character: Through Hilary's thoughts and speech, readers understand his round, complex personality.
The story follows a linear, chronological structure: events unfold in the order they happen.
Focus on a single moral dilemma: The narrative is tightly centered on Hilary's decision regarding his aunt's suffering, without major subplots.

Figurative Language

Australia did not like Hilary
Fate has found a home for him.
Hilary's sun shone again: Without money he lives in darkness but with the financial security his life becomes bright. Thus, money resembles the sun that shines and enlightens his life.
He felt like a sailor who had reached harbor: without money Hilary resembles a sailor in the sea during a storm and after securing money he became safe like a sailor who had reached harbor.
Situational Irony occurs when something happens which is totally different from what is expected. Hilary planned to get rid of his aunt before changing her will to inherit her money. The irony occurred in the last situation between them when his aunt discovered his plan and explained that if she died that night he would inherit nothing. She agreed to drink the medicine and he lost her money.
Since the plot revolves around a "mercy killing," the imagery is heavily colored by the sickroom.
Hilary perceives the world through a lens of evidence and procedure.
The "Perfect Crime": Hilary sees his plan as a perfectly constructed building where every brick must fit perfectly.
Hare uses "Cozy Mystery" tropes to create a sense of false security.
NARRATOR & POINT OF VIEW
Third-Person Limited
The story is told from a third-person perspective, but the "lens" is strictly limited to the protagonist, Hilary.
Limited Scope: The narrator does not enter the minds of the Aunt, the doctor, or the servants. This is a strategic choice by Hare; if we knew what the Aunt was thinking, the final twist would be ruined. We are forced to rely entirely on Hilary's (often arrogant) interpretation of events.

Elements of Poetry
Poetry uses language in its most concentrated form. Each element works together to create meaning, emotion, and music.

Elements of Poetry
The central idea or underlying message of the poem.

Like in fiction, theme is what the poem is really about beneath its surface subject.

The "voice" talking in the poem (not always the poet).

The speaker is a persona — a character created by the poet, much like a narrator in fiction.

The speaker's attitude toward the subject.

Poetry · Element 8
Sound Devices
Poetry is as much about sound as it is about meaning. These devices create music in language.
🎵

1. Rhyme

Definition: Repetition of similar sounds at the end of words.
🔤

2. Rhyme Scheme

Definition: The pattern of rhymes in a poem, usually labeled with letters (ABAB, AABB, etc.).
🅰️

3. Alliteration

Definition: Repetition of the initial consonant sounds in nearby words.

Example: "Peter Piper picked a peck"

🔵

4. Assonance

Definition: Repetition of vowel sounds in nearby words.

Example: "The rain in Spain stays mainly on the plain"

🔴

5. Consonance

Definition: Repetition of consonant sounds, usually at the end of words, but can be anywhere.
💥

6. Onomatopoeia

Definition: Words that imitate natural sounds.

Example: buzz, crash, sizzle, whisper, hiss

Poetry · Elements 9–10
Rhythm, Meter & Form
The physical architecture of a poem — how its lines are built, grouped, and measured.
RHYTHM & METER
9. Rhythm & Meter — The beat of the poem.
STRUCTURE & FORM
10. Structure & Form: Stanzas (groups of lines) | Line length | Enjambment (sentence continues to next line) | End-stopped lines (complete pause at line end) | Fixed forms (sonnet, haiku, free verse, etc.)
🥁

Rhythm

The musical beat or cadence of the poem — the pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables that gives poetry its flow and energy.

📏

Meter

A regular, measured pattern of rhythm. The most common in English is iambic pentameter — ten syllables per line, alternating unstressed and stressed.

📦

Stanza

A group of lines in a poem — the equivalent of a paragraph in prose. Stanzas organize the poem visually and rhythmically.

➡️

Enjambment

When a sentence or thought continues beyond the end of a line without a pause — creating momentum, urgency, or surprise.

⏸

End-Stopped Lines

Lines that end with a natural pause or punctuation mark — creating a sense of completeness, deliberateness, or weight at the end of each line.

📐

Fixed Forms

Poems with set rules: sonnet (14 lines), haiku (5-7-5 syllables), free verse (no fixed meter or rhyme).
